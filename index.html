This module is specifically about creating digital documents in Canva — designs you share via email, post to a website, or distribute as a digital file. If you are printing something and it will only ever exist as a physical copy, some of these rules are less critical. But when in doubt, designing accessibly is always the better choice.

Section 1

Use Clear and Simple Language

Accessible design starts before you open Canva. The words you choose matter as much as the colors and fonts you use. Clear language helps everyone — including people with cognitive disabilities, English language learners, and families who are skimming a busy flyer.

Avoid Jargon

Jargon is specialized language used by a particular group. A word that seems obvious to you may be meaningless — or mean something entirely different — to someone else.

✗ Avoid

Contact your student's SST for MTSS support regarding their IEP accommodations.

✓ Better

Contact your student's support team if you have questions about their learning plan.

Define Acronyms

Don't assume your audience knows what an acronym means. Write it out in full the first time, then use the short version after that.

✗ Avoid

The ADA requires equal access for all students.

✓ Better

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires equal access for all students. The ADA applies to all district communications.

Quick apply in CanvaBefore finalizing any design, read your text out loud as if explaining it to a family member who has never worked in a school. If a word or phrase would need explaining, simplify it.
Section 2

Color Contrast & Font Choices

Low contrast and hard-to-read fonts are two of the most common accessibility failures in everyday designs. Both are easy to fix once you know what to look for.

Color Contrast

Your text must stand out clearly from its background. This matters for people with low vision, color blindness, or anyone reading on a screen in bright light.

✗ Hard to read

Light gray text on light gray background fails contrast.

✓ Easy to read

Dark navy text on light blue background passes contrast.

How to change background color in Canva

  1. Click anywhere on the background of your design (not on a text box or element).

  2. Click the color swatch that appears in the top toolbar.

  3. Choose a color. Avoid gradients for text backgrounds — they create contrast problems at different points of the gradient.

How to change text color in Canva

  1. Select the text box, then highlight the text you want to change.

  2. Click the text color option in the top toolbar (the letter A with a color bar beneath it).

  3. Choose a color with sufficient contrast against your background. Dark text on white, or white text on dark — both work well.

Font Choices

Sans serif fonts — those without the small decorative tails on each letter — are generally easier to read, especially for people with visual impairments or dyslexia.

✗ Avoid for body text

Serif fonts have small tails on each letter. Use sparingly for body text.

Decorative or script fonts are very hard to read — avoid entirely for body text.

✓ Better choices

Arial — clean, no tails, widely readable.

Segoe UI — modern and easy to read at any size.

💡
Even for print designs, be thoughtful about decorative fonts. Someone may photograph your flyer and try to read it on a small screen, or a family member with a visual impairment may be trying to read it at arm's length.
Quick apply in CanvaTo change a font: select your text, click the font name in the top toolbar, and search for a sans serif option like Arial, Lato, Open Sans, or Montserrat. Use Canva's accessibility checker (Section 7) to flag contrast issues automatically.

Section 3

Headings & Structure

Headings are not just a design choice — they are a navigation tool. Screen readers use heading structure to let users jump between sections of a document. If you create a "heading" by simply making text bigger and bold, a screen reader will read it as ordinary body text with no structural meaning.

⚠️
Important: Making text larger and bold does NOT create a real heading. You must use Canva's built-in text styles to create headings that screen readers can navigate.

How to add proper headings in Canva

  1. Click Text in the left sidebar (the third option down).

  2. You will see three default options: Add a heading, Add a subheading, and Add a little bit of body text. Always start from these — not from the plain text box at the top.

  3. Click the appropriate style for your content. Heading for main titles, subheading for section labels, body text for everything else.

  4. If elements stack on top of each other, drag them into the correct visual order on the page.

ASD20 advantage: Your school's Canva brand kit includes pre-set heading styles using your official colors and fonts. When you use these, you get properly structured headings AND on-brand design at the same time.

Quick apply in CanvaBefore adding any text, ask: is this a heading, a subheading, or body text? Start with the appropriate style from the Text panel. Resize or recolor after — but never skip the semantic step.
Section 4

Reading Order

When a screen reader processes your Canva design, it reads elements in the order they were added to the canvas — not necessarily the order they appear visually on the page. A design that looks perfectly organized to a sighted reader can be completely scrambled for someone using assistive technology.

Reading order is one of the most important and most overlooked accessibility settings in Canva. The good news: it is easy to fix once you know where to find it.

How to check and fix reading order in Canva

  1. Go to File in the top menu bar.

  2. Click Accessibility.

  3. Click Reading order. A panel will open on the right side listing every element on the page.

  4. Review the order. Elements should be listed top to bottom, left to right — the same order a sighted reader would naturally follow.

  5. To fix the order, drag and drop elements in the panel into the correct sequence.

✗ Scrambled reading order

1. Body text paragraph
2. Call-to-action button
3. Main heading
4. Subheading
A screen reader follows this order — heading comes after body text.

✓ Correct reading order

1. Main heading
2. Subheading
3. Body text paragraph
4. Call-to-action button
Logical flow — a screen reader can navigate this correctly.

💡
Get in the habit of checking reading order as one of your last steps before downloading or sharing any digital design. It takes less than two minutes and makes a real difference for screen reader users.
Section 5

Alt Text for Images

Alternative text (alt text) is a written description of an image that screen readers read aloud in place of the image. Without it, a person using a screen reader hears nothing — or just a file name — when they reach your image.

How to add alt text in Canva

  1. Click the image you want to describe.

  2. Either right-click the image, or click the three dots (⋯) menu that appears.

  3. Select Alt text.

  4. Click Suggest for me to let Canva's AI generate a starting description, then review and edit it to make sure it captures the meaning.

  5. Click Save.

✗ Unhelpful alt text
imagephoto.jpgpicture of people
✓ Meaningful alt text
Students working together at a table in a school libraryBar graph showing spring food drive results by grade level, with 5th grade collecting the most items

Decorative images

If an image is purely decorative — a background pattern, a border element, a shape used for visual interest only — check the Mark as decorative box instead. This tells the screen reader to skip it. But use this sparingly: if your document has many decorative images, a screen reader user will hear "decorative" over and over, which is frustrating and unhelpful.

Quick apply in CanvaBefore adding an image, ask: does this image add information, or is it just decoration? If it adds information, it needs meaningful alt text. If it is purely decorative, mark it as such — but consider whether you really need it at all.
Section 6

Descriptive Link Text

Screen reader users often navigate a document by jumping between links — they hear only the link text itself, without the surrounding sentence. Vague link text like "click here" is meaningless without that context.

Also important: in digital documents, underline indicates a link. Bold signals emphasis. Italic signals a title or citation. Do not use underline for decoration — a screen reader user will expect it to be a clickable link.

✗ Vague link text

For the lunch menu, click here.

Read more about the spring carnival.

✓ Descriptive link text

View the Jefferson Elementary May lunch menu (PDF).

Read about the spring carnival activities and schedule.

Quick apply in CanvaBefore sharing any design with links, read only the link text — cover the surrounding sentence. Does the link still make sense on its own? If not, rewrite the anchor text itself.
Section 7

Canva's Accessibility Checker

Canva has a built-in accessibility checker that can identify common issues before you share your design. It is a helpful safety net — but it is not a replacement for your own knowledge and design choices.

Video: Canva Accessibility Tips — adding alt text, using the accessibility checker, and more. Captions available — click CC in the video player.

How to run the accessibility checker in Canva

  1. Click File in the top menu bar.

  2. Click Accessibility.

  3. Click Check design accessibility. A panel will open on the right showing any issues found.

  4. Review each issue. For color contrast issues, Canva will suggest alternative colors — you can accept a suggestion or make your own fix.

  5. For missing alt text, you can add it directly from the checker panel without locating the image manually.

✓ Checker will catch

Missing alt text on images

Color contrast failures on text

Media without captions (if video is present)

✗ Checker will NOT catch

Decorative fonts that are hard to read

Jargon or unclear language

Incorrect reading order

Vague link text

⚠️
No accessibility checker can replace your training and design judgment. Use it as a final check — not your only check. Your knowledge of this material is the most important accessibility tool you have.

Captions on media

If you embed a video in your Canva design, go to File → Accessibility and enable Show captions on media. This ensures captions display automatically for anyone who views the design with the embedded video.
